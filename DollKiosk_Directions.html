Fashion Marketing Doll Kiosk — Directions

Kellam Smile Bags · Cindy Miller · Kellam High School · Spring 2026

What You Are Doing

You are designing a paper doll outfit for a child in our community who needs a smile. You will use the Doll Kiosk to choose a doll and outfits that connect to your OneWord — then print a black and white sheet the child can color, cut out, and play with.

How to Use the Kiosk — Step by Step
1
Choose Your OneWord
Find your assigned word in the grid and click it. Read the meaning — every decision you make should connect back to this word.
2
Read Your Fashion Reference Card
A Reference Card appears showing the color palette, silhouette, style notes, and feeling for your word. Read it carefully before you touch anything else — this card was made to guide your design.
3
Choose Your Doll
Select the doll that feels right for your design. Think about who might receive this bag and choose with them in mind.
4
Open the Closet
The closet shows outfits curated for your OneWord. Select one or more outfits that best reflect your word's meaning. The child can mix and match!
5
Write Your Designer Statement
Write 3–5 sentences connecting your design choices to your OneWord. See the section below for exactly what to include.
6
Print Your Design Sheet
Click Print My Design Sheet. Your doll, outfits, and statement print as a full-page layout — ready to color, cut, and go into a Kellam Smile Bag.
Connecting Your Outfit to Your OneWord

A great designer tells a story. Use these lenses to think through your choices:

LensWhat to Think About
ColorWhat colors feel like your word? Use your Reference Card's color palette as a guide. Don't just pick your favorite color — pick the color that belongs to your word.
SilhouetteDoes the shape of the outfit reflect your word? FLOW needs something fluid. ANCHOR needs something structured. BLOOM needs something that opens outward. Match the shape to the meaning.
StyleIs your word energetic (THRIVE)? Calm (HEAL)? Bold (PERSIST)? The style should carry the same energy as the word. Check your Reference Card's Style Notes.
FeelingImagine the child opening the bag and seeing your doll. What do you want them to feel? Design backward from that feeling.
Writing Your Designer Statement

Your statement prints at the bottom of your paper doll sheet. Include all four of these:

1
Your OneWord and its meaning
In your own words — not just the kiosk definition. What does this word mean to you?
2
Your outfit choices and why
Specific connection to the word's meaning. Not "I chose it because it's cute" — tell us WHY this outfit belongs to this word.
3
Your color story
Which colors did you choose and why do they fit your word? Name them specifically.
4
Designed for the child
Who are you designing for and what feeling do you want to create? End with them — this is a gift.
Sample Statement — BLOOM

"My OneWord is BLOOM, which means opening up to something beautiful. I chose the floral dress because the full skirt mirrors how a flower opens — spreading outward from a center. The soft pink and cream palette reflects the gentle, hopeful energy of something just beginning to unfold. I designed this for a child who needs to know that beautiful things are still ahead — and I want them to feel that the moment they open this bag."

How Your Design Gets Added to the Closet

When your paper doll is complete and approved, it doesn't disappear — it gets added to the kiosk's closet, tagged to your OneWord. The next student who gets your word will see your design in the closet. Over time, the closet grows into a student-created fashion archive — every outfit made by a real Kellam student for a real child.

To have your design added

Your teacher will submit your approved design to the Instructional Technology Specialist, who will add it to the kiosk tagged to your OneWord. Future students will see your name credited next to your design.

The Vision

The Fashion Marketing Doll Kiosk is a living, growing digital experience. Students design paper doll outfits inspired by their OneWord, print them for a child in our community, and — over time — their designs are added back to the kiosk so future students can choose from a library of student-created fashion. Every class leaves something behind for the next one.

The Kiosk — Step by Step
1
Select OneWord
Student finds their assigned word in a 25-word grid. Each card shows the word and its human meaning.
2
Fashion Reference Card
Immediately after selecting, a full-color reference card appears — color palette, silhouette guidance, style notes, feeling, and a designer statement starter. Students read before touching the closet.
3
Choose a Doll
Four options: Braided Hair, Curly Hair, Short Hair, Curly Hair Boy.
4
Open the Closet
Outfits curated for the student's OneWord. As students add designs each semester, this closet grows into a student-created archive.
5
Write Designer Statement
Student writes 3-5 sentences. The kiosk prompts with a word-specific question and sample starter.
6
Print
Full-page B&W layout — doll left, outfits right, statement bottom. Print on cardstock for best results.
Conferencing Questions

As students work, use these to deepen their thinking:

QuestionWhat You're Listening For
"What colors feel like your word? Walk me through your color story."Intentional connection between palette and meaning — not just favorite colors.
"How does the shape of this outfit reflect the meaning of your word?"Structural thinking — silhouette as vocabulary.
"If the child doesn't know your word, what will the outfit tell them?"Design as communication — empathy for the recipient.
"Is the energy of this outfit matching the energy of your word?"Emotional alignment between design and meaning.
"What do you want the child to feel when they open this bag?"Purpose-driven design — starting from the recipient's experience.

How Student Designs Get Added to the Closet

This is what makes the kiosk grow over time. Here is the process:

1
Student completes & gets approval
Teacher reviews the paper doll sheet and designer statement for quality and word connection.
2
Teacher submits to ITS
Send the approved design (physical or digital) to Ms. Howell with the student's name and their OneWord.
3
ITS processes the image
Ms. Howell photographs, crops, converts to B&W, and adds the outfit to the kiosk tagged to the student's OneWord.
4
Design appears in the closet
On the next kiosk update, the student's design appears in the closet — credited by name — for future students to select.
The Long-Term Vision

Each semester the closet grows. By year three, a student assigned BLOOM opens the closet and finds 15–20 BLOOM outfits — all designed by Kellam Fashion Marketing students. They choose from a real student-created fashion archive. The project becomes self-sustaining, culturally rich, and genuinely owned by students.
