The Empathy-Driven Design FrameworkThe Philosophy Behind This Project
💜
The Core Principle
Every creative decision a student makes in this project is shaped by a single question: "What does the child who will receive this need?" Not "What do I like?" — but "What serves them?" This reorientation is the engine of the framework. It transforms art class from self-expression into empathetic service design.

In empathy-driven design, the designer's personal taste is secondary to the recipient's experience. Students are not designing for a grade — they are designing for a specific elementary-age child who will open a Smile Bag at a moment chosen by their guidance counselor. That child might be having the hardest week of their young life. The student's design is a small act of care delivered across schools, across ages, and across context.

This framework asks students to hold three things in tension throughout the project: their own creative voice, the capabilities of their tools, and the needs of their recipient. The best designs honor all three. This is exactly the tension professional designers navigate — and it is what makes this project academically rigorous as well as deeply meaningful.

⭐
Museum Quality — What It Means Here
The standard for this project is museum quality — not because the work will hang in a museum, but because the child receiving it deserves the student's genuine best. Museum quality means: intentional choices, clean execution, and work the student is proud to put their name on. It is the opposite of "good enough."

The Three Student ContributionsWhat Students Make & Why
01
AI Art Styles — Coloring Pages
Students explore the OneWord Coloring Book — comparing outputs from ChatGPT, CoPilot, and Gemini across seven distinct art styles. They select pages that match their assigned word and print them for inclusion in the bags. This contribution builds AI literacy and critical visual analysis: students must evaluate which style serves the child recipient best, not just which one they personally prefer.
Prints for bags
02
Stencil Set Design
Students design a complete stencil set for their assigned OneWord: the word itself plus 2–3 symbolic images the child can use to paint on canvas. Designs must be stencil-ready — bold, cuttable, with deliberate bridge placement for enclosed letters. The empathy challenge is real: if the design is too complex, the child cannot use it. Simplicity here is kindness.
Physical deliverable
03
Jigsaw Puzzle
Students use their digital art creation tools to prepare Set G nature poster images as jigsaw puzzles — printed, laminated, and cut into 8–20 pieces. The number of pieces should match the recipient's age and ability. This contribution pairs technical production skills with recipient awareness: a 20-piece puzzle designed for a 6-year-old is a design failure regardless of how well it was cut.
Physical deliverable
🔗
Why These Three Work Together
Each contribution builds on a different design skill: AI literacy and critical evaluation (coloring pages), concept-to-physical translation and stencil engineering (stencil set), and production craft with recipient calibration (jigsaw puzzle). Together they cover digital, analog, and production design — a complete arc for the semester.

4
Suggested PacingA Flexible Timeline

This timeline is a guide, not a prescription. Adjust based on your class pace, block vs. period scheduling, and how deeply you want to explore the AI comparison component. The contributions can run sequentially or overlap.

1
Week 1
Project Launch & Empathy Framing
Introduce the Smile Bags project and the recipient context. Who receives a bag? Why? How does knowing your recipient change your design decisions? Introduce the 25 OneWords and assign words to students.
2
Weeks 2–3
AI Art Styles Exploration & Coloring Page Selection
Students explore the OneWord Coloring Book across all seven AI art styles. Structured comparison: Which style serves a child best? Which reflects the word's meaning most powerfully? Students select and print their coloring pages for the bags.
3
Weeks 3–5
Stencil Set — Concept & Design
Students sketch symbol concepts, get teacher approval, then move to digital design tools. Build word stencil + 2–3 symbol stencils. Review for stencil-readiness: bridges, minimum stroke width, cuttability. Print test before committing to stencil film.
4
Week 5–6
Stencil Set — Cut, Test, Refine
Students cut stencils and conduct paint tests on paper. Evaluate: Are edges clean? Do shapes hold together? Can a child use this? Refine before finalizing. Artist statement written alongside cutting.
5
Weeks 6–7
Jigsaw Puzzle — Prepare, Print, Laminate, Cut
Students select their Set G nature poster image, prepare it in their digital art tool, print, laminate, and cut into 8–20 pieces. Piece count decision requires recipient age awareness — this is an explicit empathy checkpoint.
6
Week 7–8
Final Review & Bag Assembly
Quality review of all three contributions. Does each piece reflect museum-quality effort? Does it serve the recipient? Contributions submitted for bag assembly by Operation Smiles Club.
5
Assessment FrameworkEvaluating Empathy-Driven Work
📐
The Central Assessment Question
For each contribution, the primary assessment question is: "Does this serve the recipient?" Technical quality matters, but technical quality in service of the wrong outcome is still a design failure. Evaluation should always layer recipient-awareness over craft.
Coloring Pages
Did the student engage critically with the AI comparison? Was their selection intentional — can they articulate why this style serves the recipient? Is the print clean and bag-ready?
Stencil Set
Is the design stencil-ready (bridges in place, minimum stroke width, no floating islands)? Does the symbol connect meaningfully to the word? Could a child aged 6–10 successfully paint with this stencil? Is the artist statement genuine and specific?
Jigsaw Puzzle
Is the piece count appropriate for the recipient's age? Are cuts clean and pieces interlocking? Is the image printed at sufficient quality? Would this puzzle hold up to use by a child?
All Work
Does the body of work reflect museum-quality effort — not perfection, but genuine investment? Can the student articulate the empathy-driven decisions they made across all three contributions?

Future Ready ProfilePortrait of a Graduate

This project targets two Portrait of a Graduate domains. Use these as discussion anchors when students are reflecting on their work.

🔧
Problem Solvers & Value Creators
Students encounter genuine design constraints — stencils that won't cut, pieces that don't interlock, symbols that don't communicate. They must solve these problems with the tools and techniques available to them, producing work that has real-world utility.
💜
Personally & Socially Responsible
Every decision is made in service of a child the student will never meet. This is responsible design in its purest form — using skill and creativity to contribute positively to the lives of others, without recognition or reward.
💬
Reflection Prompt for Students
"Describe one moment in this project where you made a design decision based on what the child needed rather than what you personally preferred. What did you change, and why?" — This prompt surfaces the empathy-driven framework in student voice and makes an excellent portfolio artifact.
