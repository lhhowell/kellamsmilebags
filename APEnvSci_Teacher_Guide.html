01

Unit Overview

After AP testing, AP Environmental Science students take on a passion project that connects the real-world science they have been studying — cleft conditions, 3D printing filament lifecycle, acrylic mirror material analysis — to a hands-on service contribution through Operation Smiles. Over the course of the unit, each student contributes four original items that travel into Smile Bags for elementary and middle-school recipients:

  • 15 DNA Trait Cards — genetics trading cards linking a OneWord to a real human trait
  • 45 Smile Mirror Stickers — OneWord circular stickers adhered to the back of Smile Mirror discs
  • 15 Research Bookmarks — "Did You Know?" bookmarks pairing a OneWord with an environmental fact
  • 2–3 Inspirational Cards per student — handwritten notes on Smile Bags 4×4 letterhead for middle-school recipients

The first three activities are browser-based kiosks — students open the kiosk, pick a OneWord, and build. Every submission is logged to a shared tracker spreadsheet you can view at any time. The fourth activity is a handwritten card; students follow a printable writing guide and produce physical cards that Ms. Honaker collects and counts at the end of the unit.

🌿 Future Ready Attributes

This unit directly supports Thinkers & Inquirers (students apply environmental science thinking to new contexts) and Personally & Socially Responsible (students produce real artifacts for real recipients).

Learning Standards

Each kiosk integrates concepts from multiple AP Environmental Science topics. Standards references below are suggested alignments; adapt as needed for your state framework.

AP ES · Unit 5.10
Impacts of Agricultural Practices. DNA Trait Cards surface the heritability and genetic variation concepts that underpin selective breeding, GMO regulation, and biodiversity loss discussions.
AP ES · Unit 8.5
Solid Waste Disposal. Mirror Sticker production and 3D printing filament analysis invite students to track material lifecycles from raw input to disposable output.
AP ES · Unit 9.10
Sustainability. The entire Smile Bags contribution is a case study in sustainable design: reusing, repurposing, and applying science for social good.
NGSS · HS-LS3-1
Inheritance of Traits. DNA Trait Cards require students to explain dominant/recessive allele inheritance using Punnett squares — the Mendelian foundation underlying modern genetics.
NGSS · HS-ETS1-3
Engineering Design. Student-designed Mirror Stickers and hand-drawn card illustrations engage the engineering design cycle within constraints (2.5" circular format, Cricut-compatible output).
ISTE · 1.4
Innovative Designer. Students use digital tools (kiosk builders, Canva, Cricut Design Space, Google Drive) to create original artifacts that solve a real problem for a real audience.

04

The Four Activities

Three activities are self-contained browser kiosks. The fourth is a handwritten card activity with a printable guide. Students can move between them in any order, though the sequence below mirrors the content progression from individual (DNA) to group (Mirror) to audience (Bookmark) to personal message (Card).

🧬
Tab 01
DNA Trait Card
15 cards per student

Students pick a OneWord, match it to a real human trait, and generate a printable two-sided trading card with a Punnett square, OneWord connection, and genetics fact. The print layout produces two 4"×6" panels side-by-side; students cut the dashed line and glue the two pieces back-to-back to form a double-sided card.

Required output: a finished trading card. Student role: scientist + designer. Each student personalizes every card with a hand-drawn illustration, personal note, or stickers before it goes into the bag.

Open DNA Trait Card Kiosk →
🪝
Tab 02
Mirror Sticker Kiosk
45 stickers per class group

Students design 2.5" round OneWord stickers for the back of 45 Smile Mirror discs. Three workflows are supported:

  • Path 1 — Build in kiosk: pick a OneWord, icon (5 options), color (4 options), add first name, download as transparent PNG
  • Path 2 — Design external: Canva, Adobe Express, or Google Drawings (2.5" round, transparent PNG)
  • Path 3 — Hand draw: on paper, photograph overhead, upload to class Drive folder

All paths funnel into the same class Drive folder. From there, the designs import into Cricut Design Space and the machine cuts + prints the stickers on adhesive paper.

Tab 03
Research Bookmark
15 bookmarks per student

Students translate environmental research thinking into a "Did You Know?" bookmark for a middle-school recipient. Each bookmark pairs a OneWord with a real-world environmental science fact and a hands-on activity the recipient can try. Prints as a flush-sides saddle-fold — students cut once and fold to make a durable two-sided bookmark.

Tab 04
Inspirational Card
2–3 cards per student · handwritten

Students handwrite a short inspiring note for a middle-school recipient on pre-printed Smile Bags 4×4 letterhead (mascots at top, heart divider, 2026 branding — 4-up cut layout). The printable writing guide walks students through tone, the "sound like a person, not a poster" rule, a pre-flight checklist, and three example voices (short & real, OneWord-anchored, warm & honest). After writing, students embellish with doodles, colored pens, or small stickers.

Required output: 2–3 handwritten cards per student, collected physically. Student role: writer + artist. This activity has no kiosk and no tracker entry — cards are counted at collection by Ms. Honaker.

Useful filters & formulas

Because everything lives in one tab with an Activity column, you can quickly count progress per kiosk using COUNTIF formulas in any empty cell:

  • =COUNTIF(B:B,"DNATraitCard") — total DNA cards submitted
  • =COUNTIF(B:B,"MirrorSticker") — total stickers designed
  • =COUNTIF(B:B,"Bookmark") — total bookmarks completed
  • =COUNTIFS(B:B,"DNATraitCard",D:D,"StudentName") — count for a specific student

Google Sheets filter views (Data → Create a filter view) are ideal for sorting by student name or filtering by a specific activity during grading.

06

The Empty Space Is The Point

When students preview their DNA Trait Cards, they may notice significant blank space below the science content on both the front and the back panels. This is not a bug. It is the most important pedagogical feature of the unit.

🎂 Why the space exists

With 90 students each producing 15 cards, the class will generate roughly 1,350 trading cards. If every card were identical to the kiosk output, the recipient would get a printed worksheet. Real trading cards are made by a person, not a printer.

Encourage students to fill the blank space in any way that makes each card unique:

  • 🎨 Hand-draw a small illustration representing the OneWord (BLOOM becomes a flower, ROOT becomes a tree)
  • ✍️ Write a personal message to the recipient — an encouragement, a question, a short story
  • ⭐ Add stickers, doodles, signatures — anything that makes it feel personal
  • 💖 Decorate the back panel too — it is the first thing a recipient flips to

This turns 1,350 printed cards into 1,350 unique handmade gifts. The recipient gets something a person actually made for them, not just printed. That is the Smile Bags spirit in its purest form.

Classroom framing: you may want to reserve the last 10–15 minutes of each work session for personalization. Students who are done with the science content can lean fully into the art — which is where the emotional weight of the gift lives.

The same principle carries into Tab 04. The Smile Bags letterhead provides the frame; student handwriting and embellishment fill it with meaning. A handwritten card is a worksheet's opposite: it cannot be mass-produced, and the recipient feels that immediately.

Assessment Rubric

Suggested assessment criteria. Adapt weights to fit your grading system. The three kiosk activities are automatically logged, and Inspirational Cards are counted at collection — together they give a complete picture of completion without student self-reporting.

Dimension Proficient (4) Developing (3) Emerging (2)
Completion All 75 tracked items submitted (15 DNA + 45 Mirror + 15 Bookmark) and 2–3 Inspirational Cards turned in 90–99% submitted; most cards turned in Less than 90% submitted; few or no cards turned in
Scientific Accuracy Punnett squares, allele notation, and environmental facts are accurately applied and clearly explained Minor errors in allele notation or fact attribution; core science is correct Multiple factual or notation errors; evidence of copy-paste without comprehension
Craft & Care Every card is personalized with hand-drawn art, a written message, or decoration; stickers are cleanly applied Most items personalized; some obvious duplicates or rushed work Minimal personalization; items look printer-output-only
Audience Awareness Content reads as written for a specific age range (elementary or middle); language is approachable, encouraging, recipient-centered Generally accessible but occasionally reverts to AP-level vocabulary or self-focused voice Content reads as written for the student, not the recipient
Reflection Completes the unit reflection form thoughtfully, connecting the service contribution to unit content Reflection is completed but surface-level Reflection missing or superficial
📒 Grading efficiency tip

Because the tracker spreadsheet has a Student Name column, you can sort by student and see every submission at a glance. Use a filter view to grade one student at a time without scrolling through 1,350+ rows.

08

Student FAQ

Common questions students ask. Feel free to share this section directly or paraphrase in class.

How many of each do I have to make?
As a class, you are contributing 75 tracked items across the three kiosks: 15 DNA Trait Cards, 45 Mirror Stickers, and 15 Research Bookmarks. On top of that, each student handwrites 2–3 Inspirational Cards on Smile Bags letterhead. Your teacher will assign individual targets based on class size.
Do the Inspirational Cards go into the kiosk too?
No — cards are handwritten on physical letterhead and turned in to Ms. Honaker. There is no kiosk and nothing to submit online. Read the Card Writing Guide for tone tips, examples, and a pre-flight checklist.
Do I have to type my name on everything?
It is optional and encouraged. When you add your first name, the recipient knows a specific person made their card or sticker. That small personal connection is part of what makes a Smile Bag feel like a gift rather than a donation.
My OneWord is really long — like TRANSFORM. Will it fit on the sticker?
Yes. The kiosk automatically shrinks the text to fit inside the circle no matter how long the word is. It has been tested with every OneWord in the project.
Can I make my own sticker art instead of using the kiosk builder?
Absolutely. The Mirror Sticker Kiosk describes three workflows. You can use Canva, Adobe Express, Google Drawings, or even hand-draw on paper and upload a photograph. The only requirement is that the final file is a 2.5" round PNG with a transparent background, saved to the class Drive folder.
Why is there so much blank space on my DNA Trait Card?
That space is for you. Draw an illustration of your OneWord. Write a short note to the recipient. Add stickers or doodles. Every card goes into a bag for a real person — make it feel like a gift, not a worksheet.
Can I make more than the required number?
Yes, and please do. Every extra card or sticker is one more smile. Submissions have no upper limit.
What if I mess up a card? Do I throw it away?
Generate a new one. The kiosk does not limit your submissions. For paper cards, recycle the mistake and print a fresh one. For Mirror Stickers printed on adhesive paper, the materials cost more — so design carefully in the preview before downloading.
How do I know my submission went through?
The kiosks are designed to work silently. When the card renders or the PNG downloads, the submission has already been sent. Your teacher can verify anytime by opening the tracker spreadsheet.
Who receives these Smile Bags?
Elementary and middle-school students across the region who are connected with Operation Smiles. They are children who are going through medical procedures or recovery, and the bags are a small way to let them know someone is thinking of them.
