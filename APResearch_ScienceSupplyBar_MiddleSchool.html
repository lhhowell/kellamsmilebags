Science Supply Bar

Middle School Edition · Grades 6–8 · Spring 2026

How the Science Supply Bar Works

Browse the supplies below. Choose 1–3 items that connect to your OneWord and your research. In your bookmark, explain why you chose each item using your scientific reasoning. Your supplies go in the Smile Bag alongside your “Did You Know?” bookmark — giving a middle school student something real to explore and discover!

🌞
Crayons & Colored Pencils
Color is science. Light wavelengths, pigment chemistry, and the psychology of color all connect to real research. Students can use these to draw observations and diagrams.
Light · Chemistry · Psychology · Art & Design
⭐
Stickers
Adhesives involve polymer chemistry. Stickers also connect to positive reinforcement in behavioral science and reward systems studied in psychology.
Chemistry · Psychology · Behavioral Science
🏃
Play-Doh
A hands-on tool for modeling atoms, cells, landforms, and scientific structures. Perfect for building and testing three-dimensional scientific models.
Modeling · Biology · Earth Science · Chemistry
📏
Ruler
Measurement is the foundation of all science. Connects to data collection, scale, proportion, and the metric system used in scientific research worldwide.
Data · Physics · Engineering · Math
📓
Mini Notepad
Scientists record observations, hypotheses, and data in field notebooks. A notepad invites students to start thinking like a researcher in their everyday life.
Scientific Method · Any Research Area
🧭
Rubber Bands
Elasticity, potential energy, and simple machines. Rubber bands demonstrate physics concepts and are used in engineering design challenges worldwide.
Physics · Engineering · Energy · Materials Science
🌿
Seeds
Seeds represent life cycles, genetics, ecosystems, and food systems. A student can grow something real from their smile bag — one of the most powerful science connections possible.
Biology · Genetics · Environmental Science
🥇
Mirror
Reflection, light, symmetry, and optics. Mirrors connect to physics and the science of how we perceive the world. Students can explore symmetry found in nature and design.
Physics · Optics · Light · Symmetry

Science Activity Clues

Use these questions to help design the science activity on the back of your bookmark.
● Same question as elementary version     ● New question for middle school

1
What is one thing a 6th, 7th, or 8th grader could DO with your supply in 5 minutes or less?
Think hands-on. Can they measure something? Build something? Observe something? Test something? The best activities start with action, not reading.
Same as elementary
2
What real science concept does your supply connect to — use the real vocabulary?
Middle schoolers can handle real terms. Instead of just “light” say “refraction.” Instead of “plants growing” say “germination” or “photosynthesis.” Use the term and briefly explain it.
New for middle school
3
How does your activity connect to your OneWord?
This is the heart of your bookmark. If your word is FLOW, does your activity show movement or energy transfer? If your word is ADAPT, does it show change over time? Make the connection explicit — do not leave it for the reader to guess.
Same as elementary
4
What would a scientist predict BEFORE trying your activity?
Ask the student to form a hypothesis before they begin. Example: “Before you try this, predict what will happen when…” This turns your activity into a mini scientific investigation, not just a craft.
New for middle school
5
What should the student observe, record, or notice during the activity?
Give them something specific to look for. “Notice how…” or “Write down what you see when…” or “Measure and record…” Observation is the first step of the scientific method.
New for middle school
6
What is the next question a curious student might ask after trying your activity?
End with a “What if…?” or “Try this next…” prompt. This teaches middle schoolers that science never stops at one answer — every experiment raises the next question.
New for middle school

Choose supplies that feel honest to your research. A student with FLOW choosing a ruler to measure water movement is stronger than a student with BLOOM choosing stickers simply because they are available. Let your scientific reasoning guide your choice — then explain it clearly on your bookmark.

Remember: the goal is to give a middle school student something real to explore, question, and discover.
