OneWord Stencil SetDesign Guidelines
You are designing a stencil set for a child who will use it to paint on canvas.
Every decision you make is an act of empathy — design with your recipient in mind.
🌿 1 Word Stencil 🖼️ 2–3 Symbol Stencils ✂️ Clean Cuts Required 💜 Empathy-Driven Design
1
What Is a Stencil Set?The Big Picture
🎯
Your Goal
Create a set of stencils for one OneWord that a child can use to paint a personal canvas bag. Your set includes the word itself as a stencil, plus 2–3 symbolic images that connect to that word's meaning. Together, the word and symbols tell a visual story on the bag.
📦
What goes in a complete stencil set?
1 Word Stencil — the OneWord itself, lettered clearly and large enough to read at a distance.
2–3 Symbol Stencils — simple images that represent the word's meaning. These are cut shapes, not detailed drawings.
🧒
Who is this for?
An elementary-age child receiving a Smile Bag. They will hold a brush, load it with paint, and trace inside your stencil openings. If your design is too complex, the child cannot use it. Simplicity is kindness.

Design Rules for Stencil-Ready ArtThese rules make your design work
✓ Design DO
Keep shapes bold and simple — thick outlines, large open areas
Use solid silhouettes the child can fill with one brush stroke
Leave bridges (connecting strips) between cut areas so the stencil holds together
Make letters thick and clear — at least ½ inch stroke width when cut
Test your design: trace it on paper and check that all pieces stay connected
Think about negative space — the cut-out area is what gets painted
✗ Design DON'T
No tiny details — thin lines, fine textures, or small dots cannot be cut cleanly
No floating islands — if a piece of the stencil isn't connected, it will fall out
No enclosed letters without bridges — letters like O, B, D need inner bridges
No realistic illustrations — stencils are graphic, not detailed drawings
Don't make shapes too small — each symbol should be at least 2 inches across
🔤
The Letter Bridge Rule
Letters with enclosed spaces (A, B, D, O, P, Q, R) need thin bridges — small strips of uncut material connecting the inner island to the outer frame. Without bridges, the center of the letter falls out when you cut. Plan these into your design before cutting.
5
Your Design ProcessStep by step
Step 1 — Research your word. Look at the AI-generated images in the OneWord Coloring Book. What symbols did the AI choose? Do you agree? What would you do differently?
Step 2 — Sketch 3 symbol ideas. On paper, sketch at least 3 possible symbols for your word. Bold, simple, stencil-ready. No detail — just shape.
Step 3 — Choose your 2–3 best symbols. Pick the ones that are most meaningful AND most cuttable. Get teacher approval before moving to digital.
Step 4 — Create digital versions. Use your design software to build clean, high-contrast vector shapes. Black fill on white background. Each stencil on its own layer/artboard.
Step 5 — Design the word stencil. Choose a bold, readable font or hand-letter your OneWord. Check all enclosed letters for bridges. Size: large enough to read clearly on the bag.
Step 6 — Print test & check. Print at full size on regular paper. Trace the cut lines. Do all shapes hold together? Are any pieces floating? Fix before cutting the final stencil.
Step 7 — Cut your stencil. Use an X-Acto knife or Cricut on stencil film. Cut on a self-healing mat. Slow, steady strokes — accuracy matters.
Step 8 — Test paint your stencil. Place on paper and paint through it. Are the edges clean? Does the stencil hold flat? Make any final adjustments before it goes in the Smile Bag.
6
Design with EmpathyThe most important rule
💜
Who will receive your stencil set?
A child at an elementary school — someone younger than you, using a paintbrush for the first time, excited to make something beautiful. Your stencil is their tool. If it's too complicated to cut, it can't be made. If the shapes are too small, little hands can't fill them with paint. Design for them, not for yourself.
🤔
Ask yourself before you finalize:
Could a child aged 6–10 successfully paint using this stencil?
Does this symbol actually connect to the word's meaning?
Would I be proud to put this in a bag for someone who needs encouragement?
⭐
The standard for this project:
Museum quality — not because it needs to look like a museum exhibit, but because the child receiving it deserves your very best work. Every cut, every choice, every symbol: your best.
7
Submission ChecklistWhat to turn in
Digital file — final stencil designs (PDF or SVG), all pieces labeled with your OneWord and name
Word stencil — physical cut stencil of your OneWord, clean edges, no fraying
2–3 symbol stencils — physical cut stencils, each labeled on the back with your name and word
Test paint — one paper test showing each stencil used with paint, clean and legible
Artist statement — 2–3 sentences: Why these symbols? What does this word mean to the child who will receive it?
